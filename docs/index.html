## VC & FUNDING

Valar Nuclear: $450M Series C

The funding comes just months after a $130M Series A, boosting Valar's valuation to $2.0 billion. Backers include defense-tech veterans like Palmer Luckey and Palantir CTO Shyam Sankar, underlining confidence in Valar's nuclear approach to a looming energy crunch. As data-center power demand is projected to double by 2026, Valar is positioning its reactors as a pioneering solution to fill the gap.
https://techstartups.com/2026/04/01/top-startup-and-tech-funding-news-april-1-2025/

PROBLEM THEY'RE SOLVING:
AI data centers need massive, reliable power that doesn't exist in grid infrastructure. Traditional energy can't scale fast enough for AI compute demand.

DIRECT COMPETITORS:
TerraPower (Bill Gates-backed, similar SMR approach but slower timeline), NuScale Power (public SMR company but higher costs), Kairos Power (Google partnership but earlier stage), Commonwealth Fusion (fusion approach, longer timeline).

ADJACENT PROBLEMS:
Grid-scale battery storage (EnerVenue raised $300M), power transmission infrastructure (utilities struggling), cooling systems for data centers, rare earth mining for nuclear fuel.

WHAT HAPPENS IF THEY WIN:
Investor lens: Nuclear becomes the AI infrastructure play, potentially $100B+ market. Risk: regulatory delays, public perception, technical execution. Google/Microsoft could acquire or build competing solutions.
Founder lens: Enables truly autonomous AI cities, space-based data centers, removes geography constraints on compute. Would need: advanced robotics for maintenance, new regulatory frameworks, distributed nuclear expertise.

Rebellions AI Chip: $400M Pre-IPO

Rebellions, a South Korean AI chip maker, closed a massive $400 million pre-IPO round at a $2.34 billion valuation. The Seoul-based company specializes in AI inference hardware, and this infusion (raising total funding to $850M) will bankroll new products and U.S.
https://techstartups.com/2026/04/01/top-startup-and-tech-funding-news-april-1-2025/

PROBLEM THEY'RE SOLVING:
NVIDIA has 80%+ AI chip market share with extreme pricing power. Asian companies need domestic AI chip alternatives for sovereignty and cost control.

DIRECT COMPETITORS:
Cerebras (IPO'd, focus on training), Graphcore (struggling, acquired by SoftBank), SambaNova (private, similar inference focus), Groq (faster but limited memory), Chinese players like Cambricon.

ADJACENT PROBLEMS:
AI software stack optimization (need to work with chips), data center cooling/power, memory bandwidth bottlenecks, chip manufacturing capacity constraints.

WHAT HAPPENS IF THEY WIN:
Investor lens: Breaks NVIDIA duopoly, massive TAM in inference. Risks: software ecosystem lock-in to NVIDIA CUDA, geopolitical tensions, technical performance gaps.
Founder lens: Democratizes AI compute globally, enables local AI sovereignty. Would need: robust software toolchain, partnerships with cloud providers, chip fabrication at scale.

Patlytics Patent AI: $40M Series B

NEW YORK, NY, Patlytics, the AI platform for the full patent lifecycle, announced $40 million in Series B funding led by SignalFire.
https://vcnewsdaily.com/

PROBLEM THEY'RE SOLVING:
Patent research takes hundreds of lawyer hours per case, costing $50k-500k per patent dispute. Prior art searches are incomplete and slow.

DIRECT COMPETITORS:
ClearAccessIP, PatSnap (acquired by Clarivate), Derwent Innovation, traditional law firms with manual processes, Google Patents (free but limited).

ADJACENT PROBLEMS:
IP valuation for M&A, trademark monitoring, trade secrets management, R&D competitive intelligence, patent portfolio optimization.

WHAT HAPPENS IF THEY WIN:
Investor lens: $50B+ legal tech market, SaaS expansion to all IP functions. Risk: legal profession resistance, accuracy liability, Big Law building internal tools.
Founder lens: Democratizes patent system, enables small inventors to compete. Would need: global patent database access, regulatory approval for legal advice, integration with court systems.

## AI DEVELOPMENTS

Anthropic Model Context Protocol Reaches 97M Installs:
Anthropic's Model Context Protocol crossed 97 million installs in March 2026, a milestone that signals its transition from experimental standard to foundational infrastructure for building AI agents. Every major AI provider now ships MCP-compatible tooling, and the protocol has become the default mechanism by which agents connect to external tools, APIs, and data sources.
https://www.crescendo.ai/news/latest-ai-news-and-updates

This is infrastructure becoming invisible - when protocols reach this scale, they define how the entire ecosystem connects. The winner isn't the AI model, it's who controls the pipes.

Google's Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite Pricing War:
Google introduced Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite, a new efficiency-focused model delivering 2.5 faster response times and 45% faster output generation compared to earlier Gemini versions, priced at just $0.25 per million input tokens. The release reflects a growing industry shift toward making powerful AI more affordable for startups and enterprises alike, intensifying the cost-efficiency race among leading AI providers.
https://www.crescendo.ai/news/latest-ai-news-and-updates

Pricing is becoming the battlefield. At $0.25 per million tokens, this is approaching commodity pricing for what was cutting-edge 12 months ago.

OpenAI Revenue Milestone:
OpenAI has surpassed $25 billion in annualized revenue and is reportedly taking early steps toward a public listing, potentially as soon as late 2026. Rival Anthropic is approaching $19 billion in annualized revenue.
https://www.crescendo.ai/news/latest-ai-news-and-updates

These numbers make AI labs the fastest-growing companies in tech history. For context: Facebook took 8 years to reach $10B revenue.

AI Infrastructure Energy Crisis:
Reuters reports that major tech companies are putting real financial weight behind next-generation nuclear projects as they seek reliable electricity for power-hungry AI data centers. These deals are providing nuclear firms with capital and, just as importantly, a more credible commercial path at a moment when utilities and governments are scrambling to figure out how to support fast-rising data-center demand. This is one of the biggest second-order effects of the AI boom.
